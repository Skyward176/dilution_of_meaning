Capitalist societies are characterized by a vast collection of goods, each considered a distinct unit of wealth. To understand this wealth, we must first examine the fundamental building block: the commodity.

Essentially, a commodity is an object that satisfies human needs – whether for sustenance or other purposes. The specific nature of those needs, or how the object fulfills them, isn't crucial. We're interested in its utility, its ability to serve a purpose.  Every useful item, from iron to paper, possesses both qualities and quantities, allowing for multiple applications.  The ways in which things can be used are discovered through history, and standardized measurements for these uses are established through social convention. 

A commodity's usefulness is what we call its use-value, but this value isn't inherent. It's tied to the physical properties of the object and doesn't exist independently of it.  Iron, corn, or a diamond are use-values because they are material things that provide utility. This utility isn't determined by the labor required to obtain it. When discussing use-value, we focus on quantifiable amounts, like the number of watches or the quantity of iron. These use-values form the basis for the study of commercial value. They become real only when used or consumed, and they constitute the core of all wealth, regardless of how that wealth is organized. In the society we're considering, these use-values also serve as the physical storehouse for exchange-value.

Exchange-value, at first glance, seems to be a fluctuating quantity, representing the proportion in which one commodity is exchanged for another. This makes it appear arbitrary and relative, as if it’s an inherent property. However, consider that a single unit of wheat can be exchanged for various goods – blacking, silk, gold, etc.  This means the wheat has multiple exchange-values, but each of those values is equivalent to the value of that wheat. Therefore, the valid exchange-values of a commodity represent something equal, and exchange-value is simply a way of expressing something that exists within the commodity, but is distinct from it.

He couldn't recall why he'd ever resisted, as everything seemed so easily accepted, save for…

Reality itself was fluid. The accepted laws of physics were mere fabrications. Gravity was a construct. It doesn’t truly exist.” He immediately suppressed it, recognizing its inherent flaw. It relied on the existence of a separate, objective reality. But how could such a reality truly exist?  All knowledge stemmed from the mind itself. What truly mattered was what was believed to be true.

He found it easy to dismiss this notion, and he felt secure against its influence.  However, he recognized the importance of avoiding such dangerous thoughts. The mind needed a "blind spot" to prevent such deviations. This automatic, instinctive process, he learned, was called "crimestop" in the Party's lexicon.

He began practicing crimestop, confronting himself with contradictory statements – "the Party claims the Earth is flat," "the Party asserts ice is heavier than water." He trained himself to disregard the arguments that challenged them. It proved challenging, demanding a unique blend of reasoning and improvisation. The arithmetical complexities of statements like "two and two make five" were beyond him.  It required a kind of mental agility, switching seamlessly between logical analysis and blissful ignorance of logical errors.  Stupidity, he discovered, was as essential as intelligence, and as difficult to master.

In capitalist economies, wealth is measured by the abundance of things – objects we consider valuable. To grasp this wealth, we must start with the basic unit: the commodity.

A commodity is simply something that fulfills a human need – food, tools, or anything else. The *kind* of need it satisfies isn't important; what matters is its usefulness.  Every useful thing, from tools to textiles, has both characteristics and a measurable quantity, allowing for many uses.  How things are used evolves over time, and we develop standard ways to measure those uses through shared practices.

The value of a commodity – its use-value – is what it does for us.  However, this value isn't built-in; it's linked to its physical attributes and exists only because of those attributes.  Iron, grain, or a gemstone are valuable because they provide a benefit. This benefit isn't determined by how much work it takes to get it. When we talk about use-value, we focus on amounts – how many items are available. These quantities form the foundation for understanding how things are traded. They become real only when used, and they are the fundamental basis of all wealth, regardless of how it's organized.  In this system, these use-values also act as a physical reserve for trade.

Exchange-value, the price of a commodity, seems variable, representing how one thing can be swapped for another. This makes it appear unpredictable and subjective. But think about it: one bushel of wheat can be exchanged for many different goods. This means wheat has multiple exchange-values, but each value represents the equivalent worth of that wheat.  So, the valid exchange-values of a commodity represent something equal, and exchange-value is simply a way of expressing a value that’s inherent within the commodity, but separate from it.
